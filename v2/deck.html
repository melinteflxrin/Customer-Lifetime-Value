The brief

From "who might leave" to "what can we sell"

Version 1 asked
"How much do we lose if this customer leaves?"

Value = protecting what already exists (churn).

This version asks
"Which product can we sell them next — and what's it worth?"

Value = future growth potential (cross-sell).

A customer's value becomes: how likely they are to buy × what each product is worth.

What we built

A pipeline from raw customers to a ranked sales list

  • Score every customer for how likely they are to buy each of three products
  • Value each likely sale in money (propensity × product worth)
  • Recommend the single best next offer for each customer
  • Rank the whole book by cross-sell opportunity — who to call first

All of it drives an interactive dashboard and this deck.

From likelihood to value

Turning a probability into money

expected value = P(buy)  ×  product value

A 20%-likely mortgage can beat a 40%-likely deposit — because the mortgage is worth more. That trade-off is the whole point of a next-best-offer.

Note: product values are made up for this demo and clearly labelled. In a real build they come from the bank's own product economics.

Intellectual honesty

What's real vs. what's assumed

  • Real: the customers, their attributes, and the term-deposit buy/no-buy outcome
  • Real: the models, the lift, and the ranking within each product
  • Assumed: the money value of each product (labelled throughout)
  • Assumed: two loans modelled look-alike (no campaign history for them)

We also caught and fixed a real methodology trap: the data is time-ordered, which silently inverts naive model scores. Naming it is what makes the result trustworthy.

The other half

Retention is already within reach

Full value = what we can sell them × how long we keep them. This demo focused on the selling half.

  • The keeping half — a churn / retention model — we already built in an earlier prototype
  • This dataset has no churn signal, so we didn't rebuild it here — but the capability is proven and ready to plug in

Combined, the two halves give a complete forward-looking customer value.

If a bank says yes

What a real project would add

  • Real product economics replace the made-up values
  • True campaign history per product replaces look-alike modelling
  • The retention model joins in for full lifetime value
  • Time-aware validation and monitoring baked in from day one

The approach is proven here on real data — production is about their data, not a new method.
