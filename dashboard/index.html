Methodology & Definitions

Detailed explanation of the dataset, formulas, and dashboard interpretation.

Purpose

This dashboard is designed to help bank executives move beyond simple churn prediction (who is leaving) to a value-driven retention strategy (how much value we lose if they leave). We take raw customer profiles, predict their likelihood of staying with the bank, and project their future financial value over time.

1. Revenue and Cost Estimation

To calculate how much a customer is worth, we first need to estimate their annual profitability. Since we are using a proof-of-concept dataset, we engineered realistic financial proxies based on the customer's attributes:

  • Base Revenue: An estimate of the income generated by the customer, derived primarily from their account balance and partially from their salary.
    Formula: 0.04 * Balance + 0.005 * Estimated_Salary
  • Funds Transfer Pricing (FTP) Cost: The internal cost of funds for the bank, estimated as a percentage (20–30%) of the base revenue.
  • Allocated Cost: Fixed and variable costs associated with serving the customer, which increase with the number of products they hold and whether they are an active member.
  • Net Operating Margin: The raw annual profit before considering any risks.
    Formula: Base_Revenue − FTP_Cost − Allocated_Cost

2. Adjusting for Credit Risk (PD)

A customer's value must account for the risk that they might default on their obligations. We use the customer's credit score to estimate a Probability of Default (PD) Risk.

Formula: (850 − Credit_Score) / 2500 (capped between 0.5% and 25%).

We then reduce the operating margin by this risk to get the Risk-Adjusted Margin, which is the true expected annual cash flow from the customer.

3. Predicting Survival and Churn

A machine learning model predicts whether a customer will churn (leave) or stay. However, a binary "yes/no" isn't enough for financial projections. We convert the model's prediction and confidence into a continuous Survival Probability:

  • If the model predicts the customer will leave (PredictedValue = 1), their chance of staying is 1 − Confidence.
  • If the model predicts the customer will stay (PredictedValue = 0), their chance of staying is simply the Confidence.

The Churn Probability is the exact opposite (1 − Survival).

4. Multi-Year Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)

Customer value isn't just about this year's profit; it's about all future years they remain with the bank. We project the Risk-Adjusted Margin over a 5-year horizon.

Because money in the future is worth less than money today, we discount future cash flows using a 10% cost of capital (Discount Rate). We also assume the customer's survival probability applies to each subsequent year.

Final CLV Formula (Discounted Cash Flow):
CLV = Risk_Adjusted_Margin × Σt=15 (Survivalt / 1.10t)

Key Dashboard Metrics

  • Value at Risk: The total CLV that the bank expects to lose based on current churn probabilities. It answers the question: "How much portfolio value is exposed to churn?"
  • Revenue Concentration (Pareto): A visual demonstration of how a small percentage of top customers often drives the majority of the portfolio's total value.
  • Value vs. Risk Quadrant: A strategic tool mapping customers by their CLV and Churn Probability to recommend actions like "Protect & Grow" (high value, low risk) or "Intervene Now" (high value, high risk).

Notes & Caveats

  • Revenue, costs, and PD risk are engineered proxies for this proof-of-concept. In a production environment, these should be replaced with actual financial ledger and credit risk data.
  • The 5-year horizon and 10% discount rate are standard business assumptions that can be adjusted to match your institution's specific planning cycle and hurdle rates.

Methodology Notes

Risk-adjusted CLV uses a 5-year discounted cash flow with a 10% discount rate. Formula: Risk_Adjusted_Margin × Σt=15 (Survivalt / 1.1t). Risk_Adjusted_Margin = (Base_Revenue − FTP_Cost − Allocated_Cost) × (1 − PD_Risk). Survival uses the project logic: when PredictedValue = 1, S = 1 − Confidence; when PredictedValue = 0, S = Confidence.
